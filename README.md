# Sustainability in AI/ML

### Summary  
Through this project, students gain first-hand experience evaluating machine learning models beyond traditional performance metrics by incorporating resource-aware considerations such as runtime, compute usage, and emissions estimates. Students are asked to instrument or analyze ML workflows to quantify the resource implications of different modeling choices, compare models under multi-objective criteria (e.g., accuracy vs. compute), and critically assess the limitations of measurement tools.

The assignment emphasizes interpretation over raw measurement: students examine what sustainability metrics capture, what they omit, and how these limitations affect conclusions. Additionally, students engage with system-level considerations by reflecting on phenomena such as rebound effects (e.g., Jevons paradox), where efficiency improvements do not necessarily translate to reduced total environmental impact.

The assignment is inspired by prior work on “Green AI” and sustainability in computing education, and adapts these ideas into a course-integrated, technically grounded ML workflow.

## Topics  
Machine learning evaluation, sustainable AI, resource-aware computing, emissions measurement, model trade-offs, systems thinking, rebound effects.

## Audience  
Undergraduate course in machine learning, data science, or introductory AI.

## Difficulty  
Medium level: students should be familiar with basic machine learning workflows (training, evaluation, and model comparison). No prior knowledge of sustainability or emissions tracking is required, though guidance may be needed to interpret measurement outputs and reason about trade-offs.

## Strengths  
- Integrates sustainability directly into core ML evaluation practices rather than treating it as an external topic.  
- Emphasizes multi-objective reasoning (performance vs. resource cost), reflecting real-world ML decision-making.  
- Encourages critical thinking about measurement uncertainty and methodological limitations.  
- Requires minimal additional coding, making it accessible within existing course structures.  
- Promotes systems-level thinking by connecting technical decisions to broader environmental impacts.

## Weaknesses  
- Includes open-ended interpretive questions, which may yield diverse but valid responses.  
- Measurement tools (e.g., emissions estimators) may produce approximate or environment-dependent results.  
- Students may initially over-rely on quantitative outputs without fully understanding their assumptions unless explicitly guided.

## Dependencies  
- Fundamentals of Python  
- Experience working with Jupyter Notebooks  
- Standard scientific Python stack (e.g., `numpy`, `matplotlib`, `pandas`, optionally `scikit-learn`)  
- emissions tracking tools (e.g., CodeCarbon)
- Basic understanding of machine learning concepts (training, evaluation, model comparison)
